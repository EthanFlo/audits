Review Gating (Why Not To)

Review gating is the practice of selectively asking customers for reviews, typically by directing happy customers to public review sites while sending unhappy customers to a private feedback form. This strategy aims to filter out negative feedback from public view, curating a seemingly perfect online reputation. Businesses employing review gating often use email or SMS campaigns that first ask customers, "How was your experience?" and then branch based on their response.

While the intention behind review gating might seem logical for reputation management, it is a problematic practice that Google explicitly prohibits and actively penalizes. Google's guidelines require businesses to solicit reviews fairly, without discrimination or manipulation. Engaging in review gating can lead to severe consequences for a business's Google Business Profile (GBP), including removal of reviews, a penalty that reduces visibility in local search results, or even permanent suspension of the GBP listing. This directly impacts a business's ability to appear in the "snack pack" or map results, significantly hurting lead generation and customer trust.

Consider a Chicago dentist's office implementing a review strategy. Their system automatically emails patients post-appointment. If a patient rates their experience as 4 or 5 stars, the email directs them to leave a Google review. However, if a patient rates their experience as 1, 2, or 3 stars, the email only provides a link to a private "contact us" form on the dentist's website. This selective routing is a clear example of review gating, designed to prevent negative experiences from appearing on public platforms like Google Maps or Yelp, which violates Google's terms of service.

A common misconception is that it's acceptable if the private feedback form is merely an option for unhappy customers, as long as everyone can leave a public review. However, the act of directing customers differently based on their initial feedback is the core problem. Some businesses might also believe that using third-party review software makes them immune to detection, but these platforms are often sophisticated enough to flag such practices. Another mistake is thinking that Google won't notice a small number of reviews; Google's algorithms are constantly looking for patterns indicating unnatural review solicitation.

Instead of review gating, businesses should focus on ethical and effective review generation strategies:

  • Solicit All Feedback Equally: Encourage all customers, regardless of their likely sentiment, to leave public reviews on platforms like Google Business Profile. Provide a direct, unbiased link for everyone.
  • Respond to All Reviews: Engage with both positive and negative reviews promptly and professionally. Addressing criticism publicly demonstrates transparency and a commitment to customer satisfaction.
  • Use Private Feedback for Internal Improvement: Maintain an internal feedback mechanism for customers to share concerns directly. This allows you to address issues privately and improve your service, turning potentially negative experiences into positive ones before they reach public forums.
  • Focus on Service Excellence: The best way to generate positive reviews is by consistently delivering exceptional service and products. Genuine positive experiences naturally lead to good reviews.
