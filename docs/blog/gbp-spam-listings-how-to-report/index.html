How to Report Spam Listings on Google Maps (And Why You Should)

As a small local business owner, you juggle a million things. Marketing often falls to the bottom of the list, especially if you're a one-person show or have a small team. But there's one critical, often overlooked task that directly impacts your visibility and profitability on Google Maps, and it doesn't require a marketing degree or a huge budget: reporting spam listings.

Think about it: when a potential customer searches for "plumber near me" or "dentist [your city]," they're looking for a service right now. If the top results are cluttered with fake businesses, misleading information, or outright scams, it doesn't just hurt those looking for a service, it hurts legitimate businesses like yours. These spam listings steal clicks, divert potential customers, and dilute your hard-earned visibility. You might not have a marketing team, but you absolutely have the power to fight back.

This post will walk you through exactly how to identify and report spam listings on Google Maps, and crucially, explain why taking a few minutes to do this can make a significant difference to your bottom line.

Understanding the Enemy: What Exactly is Google Maps Spam?

Before you can report spam, you need to know what you're looking for. Google has strict guidelines for businesses listed on Google Maps (through what's now called Google Business Profile, or GBP), but many bad actors try to game the system. Their goal is simple: to appear higher in search results, often at your expense, to generate leads for themselves or sell those leads to others.

Here are the most common types of Google Maps spam you'll encounter:

  • Fake Business Listings: These are profiles for businesses that don't actually exist at the listed location. This could be someone operating out of their home but listing a commercial address, or even a completely fictitious business trying to generate leads.
  • Keyword Stuffing in Business Names: Google's guidelines state your business name should be your actual, registered business name. Spammers often add keywords like "Best Affordable 24/7 Emergency Plumber in [City]" to their official business name to try and rank for more terms. For example, "Joe's Plumbing" becomes "Joe's Plumbing & HVAC Repair Emergency Services."
  • Incorrect Categories: A business might list itself under an irrelevant category to appear in more searches. A locksmith might list themselves as a "Plumber" to capture plumbing leads, for instance.
  • Multiple Listings for the Same Business at One Address: Some businesses try to create several profiles for the exact same entity, sometimes with slightly different names or categories, all at the same physical location, hoping to dominate search results.
  • Fake Addresses (P.O. Boxes, Virtual Offices): A service-area business (one that visits customers, like a plumber) *can* hide their address, but businesses that serve customers at a physical location (like a salon or restaurant) *must* have a verifiable physical address where customers can visit during business hours. P.O. boxes or virtual office spaces are not acceptable for storefront businesses.
  • Fake Reviews: While not strictly a "listing" issue, profiles with a sudden influx of generic 5-star reviews (or 1-star reviews if targeting a competitor) are a strong indicator of broader spamming tactics.

Recognizing these patterns is your first step in protecting your own local search presence.

Why You Can't Afford to Ignore Spam: Your Bottom Line Depends On It

It's easy to dismiss a few spam listings as "not my problem," but that couldn't be further from the truth. Every spam listing that ranks above or alongside yours is actively taking money out of your pocket. Here’s how:

Direct Impact on Local Search Rankings and Visibility

Google Maps has limited space in the local search results (the "3-pack" or "local pack"). If that prime real estate is occupied by fake businesses, your legitimate business gets pushed down or off the first page entirely. Fewer eyeballs on your listing means fewer clicks, fewer calls, and ultimately, fewer customers.

Imagine you run a dental practice in a city where "Dentist [City Name]" is a competitive search. If three of the top ten results for that search are keyword-stuffed fake practices operating out of an unregistered home address, those three spots are effectively stolen. Your legitimate practice, with years of service and real patient reviews, now appears further down the list. Potential new patients might never even see your listing.

Dilution of Leads and Revenue Loss

Every click on a spam listing is a click that *could* have gone to your business. Let's look at a concrete example:

Concrete Example 1: The Plumber's Lost Revenue

Consider a small, independent plumbing business in a mid-sized city. This plumber typically gets 40 leads a month directly from Google Maps, converting 15 of those into jobs, with an average job value of $450. This translates to $6,750 in monthly revenue from Google Maps alone.

Now, suppose your local market has become flooded with spam. Over the past six months, you've noticed at least 15% of the top 10 Google Maps results for "plumber [city name]" are now fake businesses, often with keyword-stuffed names like "Emergency 24/7 Plumbers & Drain Cleaning Experts." These fake listings are diverting traffic.

Even if these fake listings only manage to divert 10% of the potential clicks and calls away from legitimate businesses, that's 4 fewer leads for your plumbing business per month (10% of 40). If your conversion rate holds, that's 1-2 lost jobs per month. At $450 per job, that's a direct loss of $450 to $900 in monthly revenue. Over a year, this unchecked spam could cost your business $5,400 to $10,800 annually. This isn't just a hypothetical scenario, it's a real financial hit.

This is why you can't afford to ignore spam. It's a direct threat to your income.

Erosion of Customer Trust and Brand Reputation

When customers click on a spam listing and have a bad experience (e.g., calling a non-existent number, visiting a fake address, or dealing with an unprofessional lead-gen service), it erodes their trust in Google Maps itself. This can make them wary of *all* local listings, including yours. While they might not blame your specific business, the overall perception of local search quality suffers, making it harder for honest businesses to build trust.

Your Spam-Spotting Toolkit: How to Identify Fake Listings

Now that you know what to look for and why it matters, let's get practical. Here's your checklist for identifying suspicious listings:

  1. The Business Name Test: Is the business name overly descriptive or stuffed with keywords that don't sound like a real company name? "Quality Dentistry Smiles & Implants [City] - Best Local Dentist" is a red flag. Real business names are usually much simpler, like "Smith Dental."
  2. Address Verification:
    • For storefront businesses: Does the address appear legitimate? Use Google Street View. Does the business sign match the listing? Is it a P.O. box (which Google doesn't allow for storefront businesses)?
    • For service-area businesses: While they can hide their address, if an address *is* shown, ensure it's not a residential home trying to pass as a commercial establishment if their service implies a physical storefront.
  3. Multiple Listings: Search for the business name. Do you find several listings for the exact same business at the same address, perhaps with slightly different names or phone numbers? That's typically spam.
  4. Category Check: Does the business category accurately describe the business? A restaurant won't list itself as a "car repair shop."
  5. Website & Phone Number:
    • Does the website link actually go to a legitimate website for that business, or is it a generic landing page, a broken link, or a site for an entirely different company?
    • Call the phone number. Does it connect to a real business, or is it a disconnected line, a generic voicemail, or an answering service that sounds like a lead generation company?
  6. Review Patterns: Look for suspicious review trends. A brand new listing with 50 perfect 5-star reviews in a single week, all with generic text, is highly suspicious. Conversely, a flurry of 1-star reviews targeting a competitor could also indicate spam.
  7. Photos: Are the photos generic stock images, or do they look like actual photos of the business location, staff, or work?

Concrete Example 2: The Salon's Diluted Market

Consider a town with 8 reputable hair salons, all actively serving local clients. A quick Google Maps search for "hair salon [town name]" reveals 12 results in the local pack and extended list. Of these 12, 4 are suspicious:

  • One is named "Best Haircuts & Color Experts [Town Name] Salon" (keyword stuffing).
  • Another lists a residential address, but claims to be a full-service salon open 7 days a week, with no signage visible on Street View.
  • A third shows a generic stock photo of a salon interior and lists a website that's just a blank page.
  • The fourth has 30 five-star reviews, all posted on the same day by profiles with only one review each, and the business name is "Affordable Styles & Beauty Bar [Town Name]."

These 4 fake listings are taking up valuable search real estate. If each of the 8 legitimate salons could realistically attract 2 new clients per week from Google Maps searches, and these spam listings dilute visibility by 25% (4 out of 16 potential top spots, considering a few more legitimate businesses might be just off the top 12), then each legitimate salon might lose 0.5 new clients per week. For a salon where the average service value is $80, this is a loss of $40 per week, or $2,080 annually per salon. This is a direct hit to new client acquisition and competitive advantage simply because fake listings are crowding the market.

By applying these checks, you'll quickly become adept at spotting the fakes.

The Official Takedown: A Step-by-Step Guide to Reporting Spam

Google provides a mechanism for reporting inaccurate information, and this is your primary tool for combating spam. It requires a bit of patience, but it's effective if done correctly.

Step 1: Find the Spam Listing on Google Maps

Open Google Maps (on desktop or mobile) and search for the business or the type of service that brings up the spam listing. Click on the listing you want to report.

Step 2: Initiate the "Suggest an Edit" Process

Once you're on the business's profile page (the knowledge panel on the left side of the desktop screen, or full screen on mobile):

  • Scroll down until you see the link that says "Suggest an edit". Click on it.

Step 3: Choose the Right Reason for Editing

You'll be presented with a few options. Select the one that best fits the spam:

  • "Close or remove": This is your best option for outright fake businesses, businesses that don't exist at the location, or duplicates of another listing.
  • "Change name or other details": Use this for keyword-stuffed names, incorrect categories, wrong phone numbers, or fake websites.

Step 4: Provide Specific Details and Evidence

This is the most crucial step. Don't just click "Close or remove" and submit. Google wants to see *why* you believe it's spam. The more detail you provide, the better your chances of success.

If you selected "Close or remove":

  1. You'll likely be asked for a reason. Common reasons include:
    • "Doesn't exist": For entirely fake businesses.
    • "Spam": A good general category if it's clearly an attempt to game the system.
    • "Duplicate": If it's a second listing for an identical business at the same address.
  2. In the comments section, provide all the evidence you gathered during your spam-spotting research. For example:
    • "This business does not exist at this address. Street View shows a vacant lot/residential home/another business (provide details). There is no signage for '[Business Name]'."
    • "This is a duplicate listing of '[Real Business Name]' at the exact same address, which already has a verified profile."
    • "The phone number listed (XXX-XXX-XXXX) is disconnected/leads to a generic lead generation service, not a direct business."
    • "The website link (www.fakebusiness.com) is broken/goes to an unrelated site."

If you selected "Change name or other details":

  1. You'll be able to edit specific fields.
  2. For a keyword-stuffed name, suggest the likely real, simpler business name (if you can find it) or suggest removing the extra keywords. For example, change "Best Emergency HVAC Repair & Plumbing Solutions [City]" to "HVAC Repair & Plumbing Solutions."
  3. For incorrect categories, suggest the correct, more accurate category.
  4. In the comments, explain *why* you are suggesting the change. "The name 'Best Emergency Plumber [City]' is keyword stuffing and violates Google's guidelines. The business's actual registered name is likely 'Smith Plumbing.'" or "This is listed as a 'Dentist' but their website clearly shows they are a 'Dental Supply Store,' not a practice."

Step 5: Submit Your Edit

Click "Submit." Google will review your suggestion. This process can take anywhere from a few days to several weeks. You won't always receive a notification of the outcome, but sometimes you will. Be patient.

Reporting Fake Reviews

If you encounter a listing that has obviously fake reviews (either positive for spam, or negative for a competitor), you can report those individually:

  1. Go to the business profile on Google Maps.
  2. Click on the "Reviews" section.
  3. Find the specific review you want to report.
  4. Click the three vertical dots next to the review.
  5. Select "Report review."
  6. Choose the reason (e.g., "Off-topic," "Spam," "Conflict of interest").

Beyond the Initial Report: Advanced Tactics and Escalation

Sometimes, a simple "Suggest an edit" doesn't work, especially for persistent or widespread spam. Don't give up. Here are some advanced strategies:

1. Leverage Your Google Account (Local Guide Status)

If you're an active Google Local Guide (someone who contributes reviews, photos, and edits to Maps), your "Suggest an edit" submissions might carry more weight with Google, as you've established a track record of helpful contributions. Consider becoming a Local Guide if you aren't already, but do so genuinely, not just for reporting spam.

2. The Google Business Profile Help Community Forum

This is a valuable resource. If your "Suggest an edit" isn't being acted upon, you can post in the Google Business Profile Help Community. Provide screenshots and links to the spam listing, and clearly explain what you've already tried. Product Experts (experienced volunteers) and even Google staff monitor these forums and can sometimes escalate issues.

3. Contact Google Business Profile Support Directly

If you have your *own* verified Google Business Profile, you have access to direct support from Google. You can contact them through your GBP dashboard. While their primary role is to help with *your* profile, they sometimes can assist with broader spam issues, especially if those issues are directly impacting your business. Explain clearly how the spam is affecting your business, not just that it exists.

4. The Google Business Profile Redressal Form

This is a more powerful tool, often used by SEO professionals, but available to anyone for severe or widespread spam. The Business Redressal Form is specifically designed for reporting violations of Google's guidelines. You'll need to provide detailed information about the offending listing(s), including screenshots, links, and explanations of why they violate the guidelines. This form is particularly useful for reporting:

  • Fake locations that don't exist.
  • Businesses using P.O. boxes or virtual offices for storefront listings.
  • Extensive keyword stuffing in business names across multiple listings.
  • Multiple listings for the same exact business.
This form usually gets a more dedicated review from Google's spam team, but it should be used for truly egregious cases or when other methods have failed.

5. Be Persistent and Consistent

Google receives millions of edits and reports every day. Some spam reports get reviewed quickly, others take longer. If your initial report doesn't lead to action, don't be afraid to report the listing again a week or two later, or try a different method (e.g., if "Suggest an edit" didn't work, try the Redressal Form). Consistency in reporting, especially for the most harmful spam, is key.

Reporting spam is not just about cleaning up the internet, it's about protecting your small business. Every fake listing you help remove is one less obstacle between you and your next customer. By taking a proactive approach, you're not just complaining about the problem, you're actively contributing to a fairer, more trustworthy local search ecosystem.
